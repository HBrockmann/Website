Welcome
I am Hilke
Brockmann

I study the questions that keep us up at night: Will I be happy? Will I be treated fairly? What happens when we grow old? And what does the digital revolution mean for all of us? As a sociologist, I approach these profoundly human concerns with rigorous empirical methods — and a genuine conviction that science should matter beyond the academy.

For two decades, I have been a professor at Constructor University Bremen, with research stays at Harvard, the European University Institute in Florence, and other institutions. My work has tracked happiness trajectories across entire lifetimes, examined why immigrants often report higher wellbeing than the people around them, and revealed the hidden inequalities shaping women's careers. Most recently, I am pushing into new territory: using AI-assisted text analytics, digital trace data, and computational methods to understand how we age, how societies polarize, and how technology reshapes the way we live and feel.

Communicating research is not a side project — it is central to what I do. I collaborate across disciplines and continents, supervise a lively group of PhD students, and bring sociological thinking into public debate, policy conversations, and the media whenever I can.

Selected Publications

Books & Papers

A curated selection of research across the themes of happiness, inequality, aging, and digital society — chosen for intellectual impact, originality, and accessibility.

2026

Journal Article

Females Welcome! When immigrants make the local population happy

Journal of Economic Psychology, 113 (with R. Fernandez-Urbano)

A striking finding: the arrival of immigrant women boosts subjective wellbeing in host communities. A genuinely surprising result that reframes public debates around migration and social cohesion.

2025

Journal Article

The Pursuit of Happiness at the End of Life: Research on Connection, Care, and Meaning in Hospice Settings

The Mind, 2

One of the first rigorous empirical studies of what actually makes people happy in their final months — an urgent question for aging societies everywhere. This paper opened a new research program.

2024

Edited Volume

Encyclopedia of Happiness, Quality of Life, and Subjective Wellbeing

Edward Elgar, Cheltenham (edited with R. Fernandez-Urbano)

The field's most comprehensive reference work — a landmark synthesis co-edited by Brockmann that brings together the world's leading scholars on wellbeing research. Essential for researchers, policymakers, and curious readers alike.

2018

Journal Article

Why managerial women are less happy than managerial men

Journal of Happiness Studies, 19/3: 755–779 (with A.M. Koch, A. Diederich, C. Edling)

A widely cited study that puts data behind a phenomenon many women recognize — and opens hard questions about the real costs of professional success. Accessible, provocative, and policy-relevant.

2016

Journal Article

Happy taxation: Increasing tax compliance through positive rewards?

Journal of Public Policy, 36/3: 381–406 (with L. Seelkopf, P. Genschel)

A beautifully unexpected research question — can reward-based nudges replace penalties in tax compliance? This paper bridges happiness research and public policy in a way that genuinely surprised policymakers.

2013

Edited Volume

Is More Always Better? Human Happiness and the Pursuit of Maximization

Springer, Heidelberg & New York (edited with J. Delhey)

A thought-provoking interdisciplinary inquiry into the limits of "more is better" — from wealth to ambition to information. One of the books that established Brockmann as a leading voice in happiness research.

2009

Journal Article

The China puzzle: Falling happiness in a rising economy

Journal of Happiness Studies, 10: 387–405 (with J. Delhey, C. Welzel)

A landmark paper: economic growth does not automatically produce wellbeing. This counterintuitive finding for China resonated globally and remains one of the field's most-cited studies on the happiness–GDP paradox.

2004

Journal Article

Love and death in Germany: The marital biography and its impact on mortality

Journal of Marriage & the Family, 66/3: 567–581 (with T. Klein)

An early, award-winning study using large longitudinal data to show that intimate relationships profoundly shape how long we live — still widely read, still cited, still important.

Current Research

Projects

Two research programs — each tackling a question that matters to individuals and societies, and each pushing the boundaries of sociological methods.

AI & Society 01

Augmented Democracies: AI for Civic Engagement and Democratic Decision-Making

How can AI help us deliberate better, include more voices, and govern more fairly? Together with colleagues across AI, HCI, political science, and digital democracy practice, I am co-organizing a full-day workshop at IJCAI-ECAI 2026, here in Bremen, on 17 August 2026. Our aim: to design next-generation AI systems that address democracy's deliberative trilemma — broad participation, deliberation quality, and political equality. Are you working on AI for democratic decision-making? We welcome long papers and extended abstracts across four tracks, including work in progress. Submissions close 1 June 2026 — I'd love to read your contribution.

Gender & Career — Ongoing 02

Mothers at Work: Career, Care, and the Motherhood Penalty

Becoming a mother remains one of the most consequential events in a woman's working life — reshaping her income, her career trajectory, and her wellbeing. I study the structural mechanisms behind the "motherhood penalty" and look for the policies and workplace cultures that actually make a difference.

Are you a mother who has experienced career discrimination? I am always glad to hear from women whose experiences can inform this research. Please feel free to reach out — your story matters.

Beyond the University

Outreach & Impact

Good research deserves a wide audience. I actively bring sociological findings into public debate, policy advice, and the media — because the questions I study belong to everyone.

Academic Service & Scholarly Engagement

Member of the editorial board of the Journal of Happiness Studies; reviewer for leading journals in sociology, public health, and social policy. Invited talks at Harvard's Center for European Studies, the European University Institute, and the Studienstiftung des deutschen Volkes, as well as at international sociology conferences across North America, Europe, and Asia.

Policy Consulting & Practitioner Audiences

Expert consultation for hospitals and healthcare organizations on quality assurance and age-related policy. Invited keynotes at McKinsey and the Sparkassenverband, translating sociological research on happiness, inequality, and aging for leadership audiences beyond academia.

Media & Public Debate

From the Frankfurter Rundschau to Die ZEIT, from the Economist to ZDF and ARTE, my research on happiness, inequality, and aging has reached hundreds of thousands of readers and viewers. I contribute a regular column to the Frankfurter Rundschau (Gastwirtschaft, since 2019) and welcome journalists seeking a serious sociological perspective.
